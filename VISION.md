# Vision: Village Quests

## The Destination

Villages in Minecraft were born as husks. Structures with schedules. This mod breathes life into them — a life of their own, not centered around the player.

The player who tries to loot and exploit finds the system quietly uncooperative. The player who stays, listens, and earns trust finds something they didn't expect to care about.

Done means every layer singing at once. A farmer mentions the drought while handing you work at dusk. You've been here three weeks and she's stopped explaining where things are. The quest she gives you isn't fetch-ten-wheat — it's the kind of ask that only makes sense because she trusts you now, in this weather, at this hour. You finish it and nothing flashy happens. Three days later, a letter arrives.

A player's first conversation with a villager sets the tone for the entire mod. That conversation must feel like meeting a person, not activating a system.

---

## The Lineage

Millenaire planted the feeling that a village could be a place worth caring about. Village Quests carries that feeling into modern vanilla — iron golems, raids, professions, trading, beds as POI anchors. It layers on top rather than replacing. Sneak + right-click still trades. The vanilla game is always there underneath.

## The Constellation

Village Quests is one mod in a family:

| Mod | Role |
|---|---|
| **village-quests** | The soul. Dialogue, reputation, quests, presence, lore. |
| **village-mail** | The memory. Delayed aftermath, letters, off-screen life made tangible. |
| **village-builder** | The body. Natural building expansion, physical village growth. |

Each stands alone. If one is missing, nothing breaks; something is simply quieter.

## Principles

### 1. Relationships Are the Reward

If a player is running quests for the loot, the system has failed. If they're running quests because they want to know what the farmer will say next, it's working.

Items change hands. They're the excuse for the interaction, not the point.

### 2. Contextual Uniqueness Over Procedural Volume

Not more content — deeper context. A quest from a farmer during a drought, at night, when you've been trusted for weeks, should feel fundamentally different from the same quest type under different circumstances. The depth comes from threading context — time, weather, reputation, presence history, recent events, profession — through every surface the player touches.

Core memories come from moments that feel unrepeatable. Not scripted set pieces, but systems intersecting in ways only this playthrough could produce.

### 3. Mundane Before Spectacular

The mod should feel like discovering that villagers were always this way and you just weren't paying attention. The spectacular moments — a misnomer refusal acknowledged days later, a gathering invitation after months of presence, a villager who senses something wrong with reality — land because the mundane foundation is solid. Morning greetings. Weather complaints. Being too busy to talk.

### 4. Anti-Exploitation by Design

The system is quietly uncooperative with traditional Minecraft optimization. Single active quest prevents hoarding. Reputation scales asymmetrically — easy to lose at high trust, slow to gain. Work request cooldowns prevent farming. Presence tracking detects tourists. None of this is punitive; it simply doesn't reward what it doesn't value.

### 5. Multiplayer as First-Class

Two players walk into the same village. One has been here for weeks — the butcher waves, the librarian shares gossip, the farmer has work. The other just arrived — guarded greetings, no work offered, children staring. Same village, same moment, different worlds. Every system tracks per-player. Every relationship is earned independently.

### 6. Silence Is Full, Not Empty

Quiet periods. Missed events. Villagers who don't want to talk. Gatherings that happen without the player. These are features. A village that's always performing for the player is a theme park. A village that has its own life is a community.

---

## What Success Feels Like

Over real hours of play, a player develops a routine with a village. They learn which villager is funny, which one is guarded, which one they haven't earned trust with yet. One day they refuse a misnomer quest and feel genuinely proud. Weeks later, a letter arrives referencing that refusal.

They tell someone: "There's this villager in my world..."

## Constraints

| Constraint | Stance |
|---|---|
| **Minecraft version** | 1.21.11. No chasing updates until the mod is singing. |
| **Dependencies** | Fabric API only. All companion mods are soft/optional. |
| **Contributor model** | Open source, solo developed. Gate-kept by ethos. |
| **Distribution** | Personal use for now. Public release when first impressions are protected. |
| **Scope** | No feature is worth adding if it makes existing features worse. |

---

## The Current Season

**Autumn.** The systems are grown. The architecture is sound. Major debt resolved, dead code composted. What remains is shaping, pruning, and harvesting — deepening the existing layers, not planting new ones.

The temptation is to plant new features. The season says: tend what grew.

---

## The Boundary

This mod is not a village management simulator, a progression system, a content pipeline, or a hero's journey.

This mod is a quiet argument that presence matters more than efficiency — made of small moments that accumulate into something real.
